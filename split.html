Split
The app that makes dividing expenses easy.
personal project
app design
role: UI/UX designer and researcher
timeline: 9/23-10/23
PROBLEM
Living with roommates is hard.
As someone who just started living in a roommate situation where we actually have to cook, buy groceries, household supplies, and other things that we need to split with my roommates, tracking expenses is really hard, especially in large roommate groups. We tried everything – requesting money on payment apps, only buying food for ourselves, and even this giant spreadsheet for each individual item we bought split amongst all 6 roommates, with little comprehension of who we actually had to pay. 

But eventually, all methods fell through. Requesting money was too hard to keep track of for every tiny item, and keeping items amongst ourselves led to too much food in the fridge and often, the food ended up rotting anyways due to a slower pace of use.
I knew there had to be a better way of splitting our expenses… so I designed it!
Solution
Split is a financial management tool that simplifies expense-sharing among groups so that users can easily divide costs, track contributions, and maintain financial transparency.
First, make a roommate group.
Then, add your expenses.
Expenses can be either general (rent, furniture, etc.) or itemized (groceries, meals, etc.).
For general expenses, you can edit who paid for it, as well as how to split it. You can either split it by number (specific amount), percentage, or share. You can also make the expense repeat, which could be useful for expenses like rent and utilities.
For itemized expenses, you can just scan your receipt to fill out all your items, simplifying your process! Split will automatically split the tax and tip based on how much each individual paid. You can assign each item per person by clicking on the profile picture next to each item.
See how the expenses are split!
Costs will be updated in your roommate’s balances as well as in the roommate's history. Looking through your balances, you can also see the balance breakdown, remind your group mate to pay you back, and pay back all your debt at once!
Be notified of important events.
When you remind your group mate, it shows up in their notifications, along with other notifications for when someone pays them back and when someone pays for them. 
See all your friends’ balances.
In your friends tab, you can see an overview of all your friends cross-groups and how much you owe them/they owe you overall.
See your own history!
In your profile tab, you can see your previous transactions as well as your total balance.
PROCESS
First, I had to figure out who I was designing for, so I made user personas.
I knew that the main problem I wanted to solve was the ease of splitting expenses, so I had to ideate on what scenarios would involve that need. My initial user was the roommate, someone who was rooming within a group and needed to split their expenses, but I soon realized that splitting expenses expands past just roommate groups.
The personas I developed are as follows:
The Roommate
Age: 21
Occupation: Student Worker
Story: Rachel lives in an apartment with 3 other roommates. They split the groceries by who uses what and rent, utilities, and supplies equally.
Needs:
  • Easy and efficient way to divide bills and expenses among roommates.
  • Quick notifications and reminders for upcoming bills.
  • Track expenses over time to ensure everyone pays their fair share.
  • Ability to settle debts securely and seamlessly.
The Friends
Age: 29, Friends: 26-30
Occupation: Teacher
Story: Sarah and her friends often hang out, go to concerts, dine out, or plan group events. They frequently share expenses for tickets, meals, and transportation.
Needs:
  • Easily split the cost of group activities and events.
  • Keep a record of who paid for what within the friend group.
  • Settle debts after each event or on a regular basis.
  • Ensure fairness and transparency in financial transactions among friends.
The Vacationer
Age: 26
Occupation: Software Engineer
Story: David loves traveling with friends. He frequently plans group trips to various destinations, and they share expenses like accommodation, meals, and activities.
Needs:
  • Splitting expenses with friends during trips in real-time.
  • Keeping track of who has paid for what and ensuring everyone settles their part.
  • Conveniently record expenses and divide costs evenly among the group.
  • Simplify the reimbursement process when friends owe him money for trip expenses.
After User Personas, I made the overall site map – how will the user move through the app?
From there, I moved onto low-fidelity wireframes.
I made basic flows based off of the site map, experimenting with different layouts and ways of communicating information. My flows were minimal with focus on only the actual flows. 
And from there, the visual design.
I wanted to make the theme “banana split”, but without making it too out there, as the app is still surrounding money, which is more of a serious topic. I wanted to have the color palette and logo subtly reflect the theme, while still maintaining a sense of professionalism. I debated between having a curved vs sharp visual identity, with my low-fis being more curved, but I ultimately ended on a sharper identity to embody a more serious visual identity.
And finally, high fidelities, based off of feedback.
I was able to get from 10 individuals that fit into the user personas I had previously created. The main problems included:
  1. Clarity with the change in numbers – what do those mean?
  2. Showing more of the history – I want to have more transparency with the numbers!
  3. Splitting individual items is a bit confusing – how do I assign items to individuals?
After getting feedback, I changed the flows to be more user friendly, and applied the previously curated visual design to the app.
Takeaways
Be open-minded.
I had an idea of what I wanted from the beginning, but my end result ended up being completely different from what I had imagined it to be when I started, and that’s usually the case. A lot of the feedback that I had gotten from potential users changed major parts of the flow, and rather than ignoring it in favor of my initial plans, it was vital for me to reflect and iterate. This is how things get better, and how I can improve my designs!
Think from the product side.
As a designer, its easy to get caught up in being perfect with the smaller things, but it’s also very important to think about a product’s feasibility, and design with the product goal in mind. While the details are still important, it’s essential to think about design strategy, and how design can benefit the business overall.
